Intro, Rules and Quiz

About the test

In Part 2 (open cloze) you read a short text with eight gaps. For each gap you write one word. The missing words are usually grammar words (determiners, pronouns, prepositions, conjunctions, auxiliaries, modals) or words that complete fixed phrases (e.g. in fact, in order to).

Rules

  • Read the whole sentence and the paragraph before choosing your word.
  • One word per gap – no contractions (don't = two words).
  • Spelling must be correct.
  • Watch for fixed phrases and collocations.

Identifying what type of word fits in each gap

Before you choose a word, ask: what part of speech fits here? Look at the structure of the sentence and the words around the gap.

  • Article/determiner? – Before a noun: I saw ___ cat. → a or the
  • Pronoun? – Stands for a noun or refers back: ___ is important. → It
  • Preposition? – After a verb/noun/adjective: interested ___ music → in
  • Auxiliary? – Forms tense, passive, or question: operations ___ carried out → are
  • Modal? – Before bare infinitive: You ___ book early → should
  • Conjunction/linker? – Joins clauses or ideas: ___ he was tired, he went. → Although
  • Phrasal verb particle? – Completes a verb: look ___ the problem → into
  • Comparative/superlative? – Before/after adjectives: ___ better than → much
  • Negative/inversion? – After never, only, etc.: Never ___ I seen → have
  • Fixed expression? – Part of a set phrase: in order ___ → to

Determiners (articles and quantifiers)

Articles: a, an, the. A/an for one thing in general; the when we know which one or refer back.

Quantifiers: some, any, many, much, few, little, both, all, each, every, no, none. They go before nouns and show quantity or amount.

Possessives: my, your, his, her, its, our, their. Its = belonging to it (no apostrophe). It's = it is.

Demonstratives: this, that, these, those. Check if the noun is singular or plural.

Rules

  • Use a/an before singular countable nouns when you first mention something; the when you refer back.
  • Some in positive statements; any in questions and negatives (usually).
  • Much/many: much + uncountable; many + countable plural.
  • Both + plural; each/every + singular.

Prepositions (including dependent prepositions)

Prepositions show place, time, or link a verb/noun/adjective to another word. Dependent prepositions are fixed: the word always goes with a specific preposition.

Common dependent prepositions: interested in, good at, afraid of, depend on, aim at, reason for, responsible for, famous for, compared with, similar to, different from.

Rules

  • Time: in the morning, on Monday, at 5 o'clock.
  • Place: in the room, on the wall, at the door.
  • Learn verb/noun/adjective + preposition as fixed pairs.
  • Phrases: in order to, with the aim of, by means of, in terms of.

Auxiliary verbs (including modal verbs)

Primary auxiliaries: be, have, do. Used for tense, passive, questions, negatives. Forms: is, are, was, were, been, have, has, had, do, does, did.

Modal verbs: can, could, would, will, must, should, may, might, shall. Express possibility, ability, obligation, or habit. Followed by bare infinitive (no to). No -s in third person.

Rules

  • Passive: operations are carried out – the auxiliary be matches subject and tense.
  • Questions and negatives in present/past simple: Do you …? / Does he …? / Did they …?
  • Can = ability; could = past ability or polite request; would = habitual past or polite form.
  • Must vs have to – must for obligation from the speaker; have to for external rules.

Conjunctions and linkers

Conjunctions (and, but, because, if, when, that, although, while) join clauses. Linkers (however, therefore, nevertheless, meanwhile) connect sentences or ideas.

Rules

  • Because + clause; because of + noun.
  • Although/though = despite the fact; however = but (often starts a new sentence).
  • So that + clause; in order to + infinitive.
  • Watch for that after verbs of saying/thinking: I think that …
  • Whereas, while for contrast; therefore, thus for result.

Pronouns (including relative pronouns and clauses)

Personal/demonstrative: it, they, there, this, that. Stand for a noun or refer to something. There often means "something exists": There are many reasons.

Relative pronouns: which, who, that, where, whose, whom. Start a clause that describes a noun. Which/that for things; who for people; where for places.

Rules

  • It as dummy subject: It is important to … / It seems that …
  • In relative clauses, that can often replace which or who (when not preceded by a comma).
  • Whose = belonging to; whom = object form of who (formal).

Phrasal verbs

A phrasal verb = verb + particle (e.g. look into, carry out, take off). The missing word is often the particle (preposition or adverb) that completes the verb.

Common particles: up, down, in, out, on, off, away, back, through, over, into, for, with, about.

Rules

  • Identify the verb before the gap – does it form a phrasal verb with a particle?
  • Learn common phrasal verbs: look into (investigate), carry out (perform), take up (start), give up (stop trying), set up (establish).
  • Some phrasal verbs have more than one meaning – use context.

Comparatives and superlatives

Gaps often test intensifiers or structures with comparatives and superlatives: much, far, a lot, even, still before comparatives; the before superlatives; as … as; the same … as.

Rules

  • Much/far/a lot + comparative: much better, far more.
  • Superlative: the + superlative adjective: the best, the most important.
  • As … as: as good as, as much as.
  • The same … as: the same as, the same size as.
  • Irregular: better, best; worse, worst; more, most; less, least.

Negatives and inversion

When a sentence starts with a negative or restrictive word, the subject and auxiliary can invert. The gap often needs an auxiliary: have, do, does, did, can, will, would, etc.

Trigger words: Never, hardly, scarcely, seldom, rarely, not only, only when, only then, no sooner, little, under no circumstances.

Rules

  • Never have I seen (not Never I have seen) – auxiliary goes before subject.
  • Not only did he leave – add do/does/did for present/past simple.
  • Hardly had she arrived when … – past perfect with had.
  • Only when … did I realise – the auxiliary matches the tense of the main verb.

Fixed expressions

Fixed expressions are set phrases – the words always go together. The gap completes the phrase. You need to recognise the phrase and supply the missing word.

Examples: in fact, in order to, such as, as well as, on the other hand, in spite of, in terms of, on behalf of, take turns, by means of, with regard to, for the sake of, as a result, in common with.

Rules

  • Read the whole sentence – the phrase may span the gap.
  • Learn common fixed expressions by heart – they are not guessable from grammar alone.
  • Watch for prepositions: in fact, in order to, on the other hand.
  • Some are multi-word: in order to (three words), as well as (three words).

What is an open cloze?

You read a short text with eight gaps. For each gap you write one word. The missing words are usually grammar words (articles, pronouns, prepositions, conjunctions, auxiliaries, modals) or words that complete fixed phrases (e.g. in fact, in order to).

How to find the right word

  • Read the whole sentence and the sentences around the gap.
  • Ask: what part of speech fits here? (article, pronoun, preposition, conjunction, etc.)
  • Think about grammar (e.g. subject/object, tense, passive, relative clause).
  • Watch for fixed phrases (e.g. in order to, such as, on the other hand).

About Part 2

In Part 2 you read a short text with eight gaps. For each gap you must write one word that fits. The focus is on grammar and vocabulary that often appear in the exam: articles, pronouns, prepositions, conjunctions, auxiliary and modal verbs, and linking words.

Tips

  • Read the whole sentence and the paragraph before choosing your word.
  • Think about the grammar (e.g. subject/object, tense, passive).
  • Check fixed phrases and collocations (e.g. in order to, such as).
  • Only one word per gap – no contractions like don’t count as one word.

Below you can read a short guide to word types and the most common words in Part 2. Then choose a mode and a test to start.

Word types in Part 2

Part 2 gaps often test grammar words – small words that hold the sentence together. Here is what each type means and how it is used.

Article

a, an, the – go before a noun. Use a/an for one thing in general; the when we know which one.

… is an international organisation …

Pronoun

it, they, there, this, that – stand for a noun or refer to something. There often means “something exists”.

When it comes to … / There are other factors.

Preposition

in, on, at, to, for, with, about, without – show place, time, or link a verb/noun to another word.

interested in photography … with the aim of …

Relative pronoun / adverb

which, who, that, where – start a clause that describes a noun. Which/that for things; who for people; where for places.

… an organisation which has 22 members … places where you can learn.

Auxiliary verb

be (is, are, was, were, been), have, do – used with another verb for tense, questions, or passive. Passive: “operations are carried out”.

… operations are carried out … would not be possible.

Modal verb

can, could, would, will, must, should – express possibility, ability, obligation, or habit.

… would not be possible … you should book in advance.

Conjunction

and, but, because, if, when, that – join two clauses or ideas. If/when introduce conditions or time.

… harmful if they are not disposed of …

Linking word / adverb

however, therefore, such, ever, back, earlier – link sentences or add meaning. Fixed phrases: such as, in order to.

materials such as glass … ten years earlier.

Determiner

its, their, both, this, that – go before a noun to show whose or which. Its = belonging to it.

Its head office is in Paris.

Common words in Part 2

These words appear very often in open cloze tasks. Learning them helps you spot the right type of gap quickly.

Articles & determiners

a, an, the, its, their, both, this, that, some, any

Pronouns

it, they, there, what, which, who, that, where

Prepositions

in, on, at, to, for, with, about, without, of, by

Auxiliary & modal verbs

is, are, was, were, be, been, have, has, had, do, does, did, can, could, would, will, must

Conjunctions & linkers

and, but, because, if, when, that, however, therefore, such, as

Other frequent words

so, than, ever, back, earlier, fact, order, turns (e.g. in order to, such as, take turns, in fact)

About Part 3 – Word formation

In Part 3 you read a short text with eight gaps. Next to each gap you see a word in capital letters. You must form a new word from this word (using a prefix, suffix, or both) so that it fits the gap grammatically and in meaning. Sometimes the word stays the same.

Prefixes, suffixes and roots

  • Root – the base word (e.g. agree, success). This is the word given in capitals.
  • Prefix – added at the start (e.g. dis + agree → disagree; un + happy → unhappy). Prefixes often change meaning (opposite, not, again).
  • Suffix – added at the end (e.g. success + ful → successful; quick + ly → quickly). Suffixes often change the part of speech (noun, verb, adjective, adverb).

Identifying the part of speech in the gap

Before you form the word, ask: What kind of word fits here?

  • Noun – a thing, person, or idea (often after the, a, of, or as subject/object).
  • Verb – an action or state (often after to, or with a subject).
  • Adjective – describes a noun (e.g. a ___ solution).
  • Adverb – describes a verb, adjective, or another adverb (e.g. speak ___, very ___).
